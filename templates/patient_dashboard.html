Brain Tumor

A brain tumor is a mass of abnormally growing cells in the brain or skull. It can be benign (noncancerous) or malignant (cancerous). Tumors may compress and damage brain structures, causing neurological symptoms. Brain tumors can affect adults and children and may be primary (originating in the brain) or secondary (metastatic, spreading from other organs).

Causes
  • Primary brain tumors: abnormal growth of cells within brain tissue
  • Secondary (metastatic) tumors: spread of cancer from lungs, breast, kidney, skin (melanoma), or lymphatic system
  • Genetic or hereditary factors
  • Exposure to certain chemicals or radiation (still under study)
Stages / Progression
  • Primary tumors are classified by tissue type and aggressiveness (e.g., gliomas, medulloblastomas, meningiomas)
  • Secondary tumors depend on the original cancer and its spread to the brain
  • Tumor growth may cause increasing pressure, swelling, and damage to nearby brain tissue
Symptoms
  • Headaches, often worse in the morning
  • Seizures or new-onset convulsions
  • Weakness on one side of the body
  • Speech difficulties or changes
  • Loss of coordination and balance
  • Vision changes or abnormal eye movements
  • Memory or personality changes
  • Ringing or hearing loss in one ear
Diagnosis
  • Medical history and neurological exam (reflexes, coordination, eyesight, alertness)
  • Imaging: MRI (detailed brain structure), CT scan, PET scan
  • Lumbar puncture (spinal tap) for cancer or infection detection
  • Biopsy to confirm tumor type in select cases
Treatment
  • Surgery to remove tumors when feasible, including stereotactic surgery for deep-seated tumors
  • Radiation therapy (e.g., Gamma Knife, CyberKnife) to destroy remaining tumor cells
  • Chemotherapy for specific tumors like lymphomas and medulloblastomas
  • Supportive medications: corticosteroids for swelling, anticonvulsants for seizures

Alzheimer's Disease & Dementia

Alzheimer's disease is the most common form of dementia, accounting for 60% to 80% of all dementia cases. It is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder affecting memory, cognition, behavior, and daily functioning.

Causes & Risk Factors
  • Amyloid-beta plaques and tau protein tangles in the brain
  • Brain atrophy due to neuronal damage
  • Age, genetics, and family history
  • Cardiovascular conditions and lifestyle factors
  • Mixed pathologies (overlap of Alzheimer's and other dementia types)
Stages of Alzheimer's
  • Preclinical Stage: Brain changes without noticeable symptoms
  • Mild Cognitive Impairment: Memory or thinking issues not severely affecting daily life
  • Mild Alzheimer's: Memory lapses, difficulty with planning, trouble recalling names
  • Moderate Alzheimer's: Increased confusion, difficulty recognizing loved ones, sleep changes
  • Severe Alzheimer's: Loss of ability to communicate and complete daily tasks, full-time care required
Common Symptoms
  • Memory loss disrupting daily activities
  • Difficulty planning or solving problems
  • Confusion with time or place
  • Challenges completing familiar tasks
  • Mood or personality changes
Diagnosis & MRI Role
  • MRI helps detect structural brain changes like hippocampal atrophy, ventricular enlargement, and cortical thinning
  • Differentiates Alzheimer's from other dementias (e.g., vascular, Lewy body, frontotemporal)
  • Functional MRI (fMRI) evaluates brain activity and neural network disruptions
  • Diffusion Tensor Imaging (DTI) detects early white matter changes and connectivity disruptions
  • Serial MRI scans monitor disease progression and treatment efficacy
Importance of Early Detection

Early identification allows timely interventions, symptom management, care planning, and improved quality of life for patients and caregivers.

Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive disorder of the central nervous system that affects movement. It occurs when dopamine-producing neurons in the brain die, causing muscle rigidity, tremors, slowed movement, and postural instability.

Causes
  • Death of dopamine-producing neurons in the brain
  • Genetic factors: having a parent or sibling diagnosed at a younger age increases risk
  • Environmental factors: exact triggers are not fully known
  • Age: most people are diagnosed around 70 years old; early-onset PD is rare
Stages / Progression
  • Early stage: slight tremor or stiffness, usually in one limb, "pill-rolling" tremor
  • Middle stage: tremors spread, bradykinesia (slowed movements) becomes more noticeable, posture changes, small shuffling steps
  • Advanced stage: difficulty with daily activities, balance problems, rigidity, widespread tremors, facial expression reduction
Symptoms
  • Tremor, especially at rest, often beginning in thumb and index finger
  • Muscle rigidity and stiffness
  • Slowed body movements (bradykinesia)
  • Unstable posture and balance problems
  • Shuffling walk and stooped posture
  • Reduced facial expressions, decreased blinking
  • Speech changes: soft or slurred
  • Other possible symptoms: depression, anxiety, disturbed sleep, memory loss, difficulty swallowing, constipation, bladder issues, temperature regulation problems, sexual dysfunction
Diagnosis
  • Neurological examination focusing on tremor, rigidity, movement speed, and gait
  • No single lab test; diagnosis is based on symptoms and exam
  • Improvement with PD medication supports the diagnosis
Treatment
  • Medications to increase or mimic dopamine: Levodopa/Carbidopa, MAO-B inhibitors, dopamine agonists, COMT inhibitors, anticholinergics, amantadine.
  • Advanced therapies: Levodopa-carbidopa intestinal gel, continuous apomorphine or foslevodopa-foscarbidopa infusions.
  • Surgery: Deep brain stimulation (DBS) or targeted lesioning for severe cases.
  • Supportive care: Physical and occupational therapy, plus management of symptoms like depression and sleep disturbances.

Epilepsy

Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterized by recurring seizures caused by abnormal electrical activity in the brain. Seizures can involve changes in awareness, sensations, emotions, behavior, and muscle movements.

Causes
  • Unknown in 70% of cases
  • Genetic factors: certain types run in families (juvenile myoclonic epilepsy, childhood absence epilepsy)
  • Brain abnormalities: birth defects, malformations, mesial temporal sclerosis
  • Head injuries from accidents or falls
  • Brain infections: meningitis, encephalitis, neurocysticercosis
  • Immune disorders: autoimmune attacks on brain cells
  • Metabolic disorders: conditions affecting energy production in brain cells
  • Brain conditions: tumors, strokes, abnormal blood vessels
Types / Stages
  • Focal onset seizures: begin in one part of the brain
    • Focal aware: consciousness preserved; sensory, emotional or motor changes
    • Focal impaired awareness: loss or change of consciousness; repetitive movements, staring
  • Generalized onset seizures: affect both sides of the brain
    • Absence seizures: brief staring, often in children
    • Tonic seizures: muscle stiffening, possible falls
    • Atonic seizures: sudden loss of muscle control (“drop seizures”)
    • Clonic seizures: repeated jerking movements
    • Tonic-clonic seizures: stiffening followed by rhythmic jerking, loss of consciousness
    • Myoclonic seizures: brief shock-like jerks of muscles
Symptoms
  • Recurring seizures: muscle jerks, loss of muscle tone, blank stare
  • Temporary confusion, slowed thinking, or problems with communication
  • Sensory changes: hearing, vision, taste, smell, numbness or tingling
  • Emotional or psychic symptoms: fear, déjà vu, anxiety
  • Autonomic signs: upset stomach, goosebumps, waves of heat or cold
  • Repetitive movements: lip-smacking, chewing, hand rubbing
Diagnosis
  • Based on at least two unprovoked seizures occurring 24+ hours apart
  • Medical history, eyewitness accounts, physical and neurological exam
  • Tests:
    • Electroencephalography (EEG) to detect abnormal brain electrical activity
    • Brain imaging (MRI) to check for tumors, lesions, or vascular abnormalities
Treatment & Management
  • Medications: Anti-seizure drugs (choice depends on seizure type, age, response, other conditions)
  • Diet therapy: Ketogenic or modified Atkins diets for children with drug-resistant epilepsy
  • Surgery or devices: Removal of abnormal tissue, disconnecting fiber bundles, radiosurgery, or neuromodulation devices
  • Managing triggers: stress reduction, proper sleep, avoiding alcohol, maintaining healthy diet and hydration
  • Emergency measures: call 911 for seizures lasting >5 minutes, repeated seizures, or if injuries occur

Stroke

A stroke occurs when the blood supply to part of the brain is blocked or reduced, depriving brain tissue of oxygen and nutrients. Brain cells begin to die within minutes. Strokes are a medical emergency; immediate treatment can reduce brain damage and complications.

Types of Stroke
  • Ischemic Stroke: Caused by blocked or narrowed brain blood vessels, often due to clots or fatty deposits.
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke: Caused by a blood vessel in the brain leaking or rupturing, increasing pressure on brain cells.
  • Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA): A temporary, ministroke-like event caused by brief blockage, without permanent damage.
Symptoms
  • Trouble speaking or understanding speech
  • Numbness, weakness, or paralysis, usually on one side of the body
  • Vision problems in one or both eyes
  • Sudden severe headache, sometimes with vomiting or dizziness
  • Loss of balance or coordination
  • Think "FAST": Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 911
Causes
  • Ischemic stroke: Blocked arteries due to clots or fatty deposits
  • Hemorrhagic stroke: Blood vessel rupture, high blood pressure, aneurysms, trauma, or vascular abnormalities
  • Transient ischemic attack: Temporary blockage of blood flow to the brain
Risk Factors
  • High blood pressure, high cholesterol, and diabetes
  • Obesity, physical inactivity, smoking, heavy alcohol use
  • Heart disease, irregular heartbeat (atrial fibrillation)
  • Family history of stroke, prior TIA, or heart attack
  • Age ≥55, male sex, certain racial/ethnic groups
  • Hormone therapy or birth control pills
  • Recent COVID-19 infection may increase ischemic stroke risk
Complications
  • Paralysis or loss of muscle control, often on one side
  • Speech, swallowing, or language difficulties
  • Memory, thinking, or reasoning problems
  • Emotional changes, depression, or mood swings
  • Pain, numbness, or tingling in affected areas
  • Changes in behavior or self-care ability
Prevention & Healthy Lifestyle
  • Control blood pressure and cholesterol levels
  • Quit smoking and avoid illicit drugs
  • Maintain healthy weight and exercise regularly
  • Eat a diet rich in fruits, vegetables, and whole grains
  • Drink alcohol only in moderation
  • Manage diabetes and treat sleep apnea
Preventive & Medical Treatments
  • Anti-platelet drugs (e.g., aspirin, clopidogrel) to prevent clots
  • Anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin, heparin, dabigatran, rivaroxaban) to reduce blood clotting
  • Immediate emergency care for ischemic or hemorrhagic stroke

Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Multiple sclerosis is a chronic disease in which the immune system attacks the protective myelin covering of nerve fibers. This disrupts communication between the brain and the rest of the body and can lead to permanent nerve damage over time.

Causes

The exact cause of MS is unknown. It is considered an immune-mediated disease where the body’s immune system attacks myelin, the protective fatty layer around nerves, slowing or blocking nerve signals.

Risk Factors
  • Age: Most commonly between 20–40 years old
  • Sex: Women are 2–3 times more likely than men
  • Family history: Higher risk if a parent or sibling has MS
  • Infections: Linked to viruses like Epstein-Barr
  • Race: Highest risk in people of Northern European descent
  • Climate: More common in temperate climates
  • Vitamin D deficiency or low sunlight exposure
  • Obesity, smoking, other autoimmune diseases, and gut microbiome differences
Types of MS
  • Relapsing-remitting MS: Periods of new or worsening symptoms (relapses) followed by remission
  • Secondary-progressive MS: Steady progression of symptoms after relapsing-remitting MS, with or without remission
  • Primary-progressive MS: Gradual progression of symptoms from onset without relapses
  • Clinically isolated syndrome: First episode affecting myelin, may later be diagnosed as MS
  • Radiologically isolated syndrome: MRI findings suggest MS without classic symptoms
Symptoms
  • Numbness or tingling in limbs
  • Electric-shock sensations with neck movement (Lhermitte sign)
  • Weakness and lack of coordination
  • Trouble walking or complete immobility
  • Partial or complete loss of vision, double or blurry vision
  • Dizziness, vertigo, and balance problems
  • Fatigue, slurred speech, memory or cognitive issues
  • Trouble with sexual, bowel, or bladder function
  • Mood changes like depression or anxiety
Complications
  • Muscle stiffness or spasms
  • Severe weakness or paralysis
  • Seizures (rare)
  • Bladder, bowel, or sexual dysfunction
  • Memory, thinking, or cognitive issues
  • Mood disorders such as depression or anxiety
Diagnosis & Treatment
  • Diagnosis: Based on medical history, neurological exam, MRI, and sometimes lumbar puncture
  • Treatment: No cure exists, but options include medications to slow disease progression, manage relapses, and alleviate symptoms
  • Physical therapy, lifestyle changes, and symptom-specific therapies can improve quality of life
